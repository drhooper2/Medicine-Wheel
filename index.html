Indigenous Perspective in the B.C. Curriculum

Incorporating Indigenous teachings into classrooms in British Columbia is essential for fostering respect, understanding, and reconciliation. These teachings offer rich cultural knowledge, diverse worldviews, and holistic approaches to learning that benefit all students. Tools like the Medicine Wheel introduce learners to the interconnectedness of the physical, mental, emotional, and spiritual aspects of life, encouraging balance and self-awareness. Including Indigenous perspectives not only aligns with B.C.’s curriculum goals of honoring First Peoples Principles of Learning but also helps create inclusive spaces where Indigenous students feel seen and valued. Ultimately, these teachings enrich educational experiences and help build more respectful and informed communities.


The Medicine Wheel is a powerful symbol and teaching tool used by many Indigenous cultures across North America, though its specific meanings and interpretations can vary by Nation and region. Despite these differences, the core concepts of the Medicine Wheel—such as balance, interconnectedness, and the importance of the mental, physical, emotional, and spiritual aspects of life—offer valuable lessons for everyone. By learning from the teachings of the Medicine Wheel, we can deepen our understanding of Indigenous worldviews, foster greater respect for the land and each other, and promote holistic well-being in ourselves and our communities. Embracing these teachings, with humility and respect, supports a more inclusive and meaningful approach to education and life.


The Medicine Wheel is an important symbol used by many Indigenous peoples across North America. While the teachings can be a little different depending on the community, they all share similar ideas about balance, respect, and how everything in life is connected. The Medicine Wheel teaches us to care for our mind, body, heart, and spirit. Even if we're not from an Indigenous background, we can still learn a lot from these teachings. They help us understand different ways of thinking and remind us to live in a balanced and respectful way.

First Peoples Principles of Learning

The First Peoples Principles of Learning (FPPL) were developed by Indigenous educators and knowledge keepers in British Columbia. They reflect Indigenous ways of knowing, being, and teaching, and they guide educators in integrating Indigenous perspectives into learning environments. These principles emphasize the holistic, experiential, and relational aspects of learning.


  • Learning ultimately supports the well-being of the community, the land,
    the spirits, and the ancestors.
  • Learning is holistic, reflective, experiential, and relational
    (focused on connectedness, or reciprocal relationships, and a sense
    of place).
  • Learning involves recognizing the consequences of one’s actions.
  • Learning involves generational roles and responsibilities.
  • Learning recognized the role of Indigenous Knowledge.
  • Learning is embedded in memory, history, and story.
  • Learning involves patience and time.
  • Learning requires exploration of one’s identity.
  • Learning involves recognizing that some knowledge is
    sacred and only shared with permission and/or in certain situations.


It is always important to honour and respect cultural protocol.

Lesson Plans

Introduction to the Medicine Wheel

Grade Level: K-1
Duration: 60-75 minutes
Subjects: Art Education, Social Studies
Location: Classroom & Outdoors

Big Ideas (BC Curriculum):

  • Stories and traditions help us learn about ourselves, our families, and our communities.
  • People connect to others and share who they are through stories and traditions.

First Peoples Principles of Learning:

  • Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational.
  • Learning involves recognizing the consequences of one’s actions.
  • Learning involves generational roles and responsibilities.

Learning Objectives:

Students will:

  • Learn the four parts of the Medicine Wheel (mental, emotional, physical, and spiritual).
  • Recognize the circular and color-coded design of the Medicine Wheel.
  • Reflect on how each part connects to their own lives.
  • Practice respect and curiosity when learning about Indigenous knowledge.

Materials:

  • Large poster or digital display of the Medicine Wheel
  • Blank Medicine Wheel Template
  • Crayons/markers in white, yellow, red, and black
  • Picture books or short video introducing the Medicine Wheel (e.g., The Medicine Wheel: Stories of a Hoop Dancer, Teddy Anderson).
  • Small talking stick (or other object) or sharing item (optional for circle time)

Lesson Activities:

1: Welcome Circle (5–10 min)

  • Begin with a land acknowledgment.
  • Sit in a circle and discuss how we are all connected.
  • Introduce the Medicine Wheel as a symbol of balance and well-being.

2: Introduction to the Medicine Wheel (10 min)

  • Show the Medicine Wheel image and explain the four parts:
    • White (North)
    • Yellow (East)
    • Red (South)
    • Black (West)
  • Discuss how all parts are equally important and interconnected.


3: Story or Video (15-30 margin-right)

  • Read a story or show a short video about the Medicine Wheel or Indigenous teachings.

4: Hands-On Activity (10–15 min)
  • Give students a blank Medicine Wheel worksheet.
  • Guide them to color each section with the correct color and label it with the direction.
  • Ask students to draw something in each section that makes them think of the colours (white, yellow, red and black).

5: Sharing Circle (5–10 min)
  • Invite students to share one part of their wheel.
  • Use a talking stick (or other object) to encourage respectful listening and speaking.

Assessment & Extension

Assessment:

  • Check for understanding through completed Medicine Wheel worksheets.

Extensions:

  • Use the Medicine Wheel for emotional check-ins.
  • Add songs or drumming activities.
  • Make a class Medicine Wheel that includes everyone’s ideas.

Exploring the Medicine Wheel

Grade Level: 2-3
Duration: 75-90 minutes
Subjects: Art Education, Social Studies
Location: Classroom & Outdoors

Big Ideas (BC Curriculum):

  • Indigenous knowledge is passed down through oral history, traditions, and collective memory.
  • Learning about ourselves and others helps us build respectful relationships.
  • Stories and traditions connect us to the past and help us understand our place in the world.

First Peoples Principles of Learning:

  • Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational.
  • Learning involves recognizing the consequences of one’s actions.
  • Learning recognizes the role of Indigenous knowledge systems and worldviews.

Learning Objectives:

Students will:

  • Identify and explain the four quadrants of the Medicine Wheel.
  • Show respect and curiosity when learning from Indigenous teachings.

Materials:

  • Poster or projected image of the Medicine Wheel
  • Blank Medicine Wheel Template
  • Crayons/markers in white, yellow, red, and black
  • Journals or reflection sheets
  • Picture books or short video introducing the Medicine Wheel (e.g., The Medicine Wheel: Stories of a Hoop Dancer, Teddy Anderson).
  • Small talking stick (or other object) or sharing item (optional for circle time)

Lesson Activities:

1: Opening Discussion (15 min)

Sit in a circle:

  • Begin with a land acknowledgment.
  • Ask students: "What do you know about the Medicine Wheel?"
  • Talk about the four seasons and how they are represented on the Medicine Wheel.
  • Connect each quadrant to a season (e.g., spring is yellow, and the sun comes up in the east; summer is red, and there are beautiful sunsets and warm weather in the south; in the fall the clocks turn back an hour and it gets dark (black) earlier; winter in the north, lots of white snow).
  • Depending on students understanding, or what they remember, you may need to remind them that the Medicine Wheel is used by many Indigenous communities as a guide for balance and well-being (and there are many interpretations across the nations).

2: Teach the Four Quadrants (10–15 min)

  • Discuss each part of the wheel:
    • White (North): Winter
    • Yellow (East): Spring
    • Red (South): Summer
    • Black (West): Fall
  • Ask students what they, and their families, do during the different seasons.


3: Story Connection (10–15 min)

  • Read an Indigenous story or watch a short video to learn more about the Medicine Wheel.
  • Prompt students to think about which parts of the Medicine Wheel they noticed in the story.

4: Creative Activity (20-30 min)
  • Brainstorm as a class, or in small groups, what they do during the different seasons.
  • Students complete a Medicine Wheel worksheet: label each quadrant and write or draw an example of something they do during the different seasons.

5: Reflection and Sharing Circle (20min)
  • Invite students to share their reflections with the class.

Assessment & Extension

Assessment:

  • Participation in discussion and sharing
  • Completed Medicine Wheel worksheet with personal connections
  • Journal or reflection entries showing understanding of concepts covered

Extensions:

  • Integrate art by making a large collaborative classroom Medicine Wheel.

Deepening Understanding of the Medicine Wheel

Grade Level: 4-5
Duration: 60-90 minutes
Subjects: Art Education, Social Studies
Location: Classroom & Outdoors

Big Ideas (BC Curriculum):

  • Indigenous peoples have rich cultural traditions and perspectives passed down through oral history, traditions, and collective memory.
  • Exploring different worldviews helps us develop empathy, respect, and understanding of others.
  • Well-being includes physical, mental, emotional, and spiritual aspects.

First Peoples Principles of Learning:

  • Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational.
  • Learning involves generational roles and responsibilities.
  • Learning involves recognizing the consequences of one’s actions.
  • Learning recognizes Indigenous knowledge systems and worldviews.

Learning Objectives:

Students will:

  • Explain the symbolism and teachings of the Medicine Wheel.
  • Connect each quadrant of the wheel to personal experiences and community well-being.
  • Understand the cyclical nature of time and seasons in Indigenous worldviews.
  • Engage in respectful discussion of Indigenous knowledge.

Materials:

  • Visual of the Medicine Wheel with labeled directions, colors, seasons, and meanings
  • Blank Medicine Wheel Template
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Markers or colored pencils
  • Picture books or short video on the Medicine Wheel
    (e.g., The Medicine Wheel: Stories of a Hoop Dancer - by Teddy Anderson;
    Shin chi's Canoe     - by Nicole Campbell).
  • Reflection journal or writing paper
  • Small talking stick (or other object) or sharing item (optional for circle time)

Lesson Activities:

1: Introduction & Land Acknowledgment (5–10 min)

Sit in a circle:

  • Begin with a land acknowledgment.
  • Ask students: "What do you know about Indigenous teachings or ways of understanding the world?"
    "What do you know about the Medicine Wheel?"
  • Introduce the Medicine Wheel as a holistic model used in many Indigenous cultures.
  • Depending on students understanding, or what they remember, you may need to remind them that the Medicine Wheel is used by many Indigenous communities as a guide for balance and well-being (and there are many interpretations across the nations).

2: Teaching the Medicine Wheel (15–20 min)

  • Show a detailed image of the Medicine Wheel with four quadrants:
    • White (North - Winter - Spiritual)
    • Yellow (East - Spring - Mental)
    • Red (South - Summer - Emotional)
    • Black (West - Fall - Physical)
  • Discuss how each quadrant relates to life, growth, and balance.
  • Help students understand the four parts of wellness in age-appropriate language:
    • Spiritual: Feeling connected to the world, nature, and your own sense of purpose.
      It can include quiet time, gratitude, or feeling peaceful
    • Mental: Using your brain to learn, solve problems, and make decisions.
      Reading, learning something new, or thinking carefully are ways we grow mentally.
    • Emotional: Understanding and expressing your feelings in a healthy way.
      Talking about emotions, helping others, or calming down when upset shows emotional strength.
    • Physical: Taking care of your body by being active, eating well, getting rest, and staying clean.
      Activities like playing outside or sports support physical health.
  • Explain that different Nations may interpret the wheel differently.


3:Story Connection (15–20 min)

  • Read or view an Indigenous story that reflects balance, growth, or seasonal cycles.
  • Prompt discussion: "Which parts of the Medicine Wheel can we see in this story?"

4: Student Activity (20–30 min)
  • Brainstorm as a class, what they might do to maintain balance in the spiritual, mental, emotional and physical areas of their lives.
  • Students complete a Medicine Wheel template: label each quadrant and write or draw an example of something they do maintain a healthy lifestyle in each of the quadrants.
  • Option: Have students write a short paragraph about the importance of balance in their life.

5: Reflection and Sharing Circle (20min)
  • Invite students to share insights or something new they learned.
  • Encourage respectful listening and acknowledgment of different perspectives.

Assessment & Extension

Assessment:

  • Participation in discussion, reflection and sharing
  • Completed Medicine Wheel worksheet with personal connections
  • Journal or reflection entries showing understanding of concepts covered

Extensions:

  • Create a classroom mural or collaborative wheel with shared ideas.
  • Integrate science or health topics (e.g., body systems, emotional regulation) through the lens of the Medicine Wheel.

Understanding the Medicine Wheel

Grade Level: 6-7
Duration: 1–2 class periods (60–90 mins total)
Subjects: Art Education, Social Studies, Health & Physical Education
Location: Classroom & Outdoors

Big Ideas (BC Curriculum):

  • Learning about Indigenous perspectives helps us understand the importance of balance and respect in our lives and communities.
  • Systems and ideas from Indigenous cultures offer holistic approaches to health, identity, and interconnectedness.

First Peoples Principles of Learning:

  • Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational.
  • Learning involves generational roles and responsibilities.
  • Learning recognizes Indigenous knowledge systems and worldviews.

Learning Objectives:

Students will:

  • Understand the four quadrants of the Medicine Wheel (mental, physical, emotional, spiritual).
  • Explore how the Medicine Wheel is used to support well-being and balance.
  • Reflect on how these teachings relate to their own lives.
  • Respectfully engage with Indigenous worldviews and teachings.

Materials:

  • Visual of the Medicine Wheel with labeled directions, colors, seasons, and meanings
  • Blank Medicine Wheel Template
  • Chart paper or whiteboard
  • Markers or colored pencils
  • Reflection journal or writing paper
  • Small talking stick (or other object) or sharing item (optional for circle time)

Lesson Activities:

1: Opening Circle / Introduction (10–15 min)

Sit in a circle:

  • Begin with a respectful land acknowledgment (challenge students to come up with a meaningful, unconventional, land acknowledgment).
  • Ask students: “What does being healthy mean to you?” Write student ideas on the board.
  • Introduce the Medicine Wheel as a holistic way many Indigenous cultures view health and balance.
  • Depending on students understanding, or what they remember, you may need to remind them that the Medicine Wheel is used by many Indigenous communities as a guide for balance and well-being (and there are many interpretations across the nations).

2: Explore the Medicine Wheel (15–20 min)

  • Show a visual of the Medicine Wheel (with white on top, yellow on right, red on bottom, black on left).
  • Discuss the meaning of each quadrant:
    • White (Spiritual) – beliefs, connection to something greater
    • Yellow (Mental) – thinking, learning
    • Red (Physical) – body, activity
    • Black (Emotional) – feelings, relationships
  • Emphasize that meanings and interpretations can vary by Nation and that this is just one version.
  • Explain that different Nations may interpret the wheel differently.


3: Student Reflection Activity (20–30 min)

  • Brainstorm and record students ideas on how they take care of their own body, mind, emotions and spiritual needs. (Remember that spiritual is not necessarily religion.)
  • Provide students with a blank Medicine Wheel template.
  • Ask them to draw or write examples of how they take care of their body, mind, emotions, and spirit.
  • Share in partners or small groups (optional sharing with class).

4: Wrap-Up Discussion (10 min)
  • What did you learn from the Medicine Wheel today?
  • How can this way of thinking help us stay balanced and healthy?
  • Option: Have students write a short paragraph about what they discovered about balance in their life in relation to the Medicine Wheel.

Assessment & Extension

Assessment:

  • Observation during discussion and sharing
  • Completion and thoughtfulness in Medicine Wheel reflection
  • Optional: Exit slip → “One thing I learned from the Medicine Wheel is...”

Extensions:

  • Invite an Indigenous Elder or Knowledge Keeper to speak (with appropriate protocols).
  • Create a classroom “Wellness Wheel” mural.
  • Connect with Science or Health units on systems of the body, mental wellness, or emotional regulation.
