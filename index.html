Indigenous Perspective in the B.C. Curriculum

Incorporating Indigenous teachings into classrooms in British Columbia is essential for fostering respect, understanding, and reconciliation. These teachings offer rich cultural knowledge, diverse worldviews, and holistic approaches to learning that benefit all students. Tools like the Medicine Wheel introduce learners to the interconnectedness of the physical, mental, emotional, and spiritual aspects of life, encouraging balance and self-awareness. Including Indigenous perspectives not only aligns with B.C.â€™s curriculum goals of honoring First Peoples Principles of Learning but also helps create inclusive spaces where Indigenous students feel seen and valued. Ultimately, these teachings enrich educational experiences and help build more respectful and informed communities.

Despite these differences, the core concepts of the Medicine Wheelâ€”such as balance, interconnectedness, and the importance of the mental, physical, emotional, and spiritual aspects of lifeâ€”offer valuable lessons for everyone. Embracing these teachings, with humility and respect, supports a more inclusive and meaningful approach to education and life.

First Peoples Principles of Learning

The First Peoples Principles of Learning (FPPL) were developed by Indigenous educators and knowledge keepers in British Columbia. They reflect Indigenous ways of knowing, being, and teaching, and they guide educators in integrating Indigenous perspectives into learning environments. These principles emphasize the holistic, experiential, and relational aspects of learning.


  • Learning ultimately supports the well-being of the community, the land,
    the spirits, and the ancestors.
  • Learning is holistic, reflective, experiential, and relational
    (focused on connectedness, or reciprocal relationships, and a sense
    of place).
  • Learning involves recognizing the consequences of oneâ€™s actions.
  • Learning involves generational roles and responsibilities.
  • Learning recognized the role of Indigenous Knowledge.
  • Learning is embedded in memory, history, and story.
  • Learning involves patience and time.
  • Learning requires exploration of oneâ€™s identity.
  • Learning involves recognizing that some knowledge is
    sacred and only shared with permission and/or in certain situations.

Lesson Plans

Honouring Traditions: Exploring Indigenous Arts and Cultural Expressions

Grade Level: Grades 4-6
Duration: 3 - 5 sessions, 50 - 60 minutes each
Subjects: Art Education, Social Studies
Location: Classroom & Outdoors

Big Ideas (BC Curriculum):

  • Creative expression is a means to explore and share one's identity and sense of belonging.
  • Dance, drama, music, and visual arts are each unique languages for creating and communicating.
  • Artistic expressions differ across time and place.

Learning Objectives:

By the end of this lesson, students will:

  • understand the role of Indigenous art in cultural traditions, ceremonies, and daily life.
  • identify and describe different forms of Indigenous artistic expression (visual art, music, dance, storytelling).
  • create an artwork, dance, or song inspired by traditional or contemporary Indigenous practices, respecting cultural protocols.
  • reflect on the significance and meaning of their creative process and the inspiration behind their work.

Visit Hello BC Indigenous Tourism for information on Indigenous arts and culture


Lesson Activities:

Session 1: Introduction to Indigenous arts and Cultural Traditions

Materials: Images, videos, guest speaker (optional), books on Indigenous art (e.g., Raven Tales, The Spirit of Haida Gwaii)

  • Discussion Starter: Ask students what they know about Indigenous art (e.g., Raven Tales, The Spirit of Haida Gwaii)
  • Showcase Examples: Share examples of traditional dances (e.g., the Jingle Dress Dance), songs (e.g., welcome songs), stories (e.g., creation stories), and objects (e.g., bentwood boxes, drums, beadwork).
  • Discuss Cultural Significance: Explain the purpose of each (e.g., masks for ceremonies, drums for communication and celebration, stories for passing knowledge).
  • Guest Speaker or Video: Invite an Indigenous artist/knowledge keeper, or show a video of traditional practices (ensure cultural appropriateness and permission).
  • Reflection Journal: Students write or draw something they found interesting or meaningful.

Session 2: Deep Dive into Visual Arts and Object-Making

Materials: Natural materials (paper, beads, leather, string, paint), images, art supplies

Focus: Traditional objects (e.g., drums, woven baskets, beadwork) and their purposes

  • Explore Symbolism: Discuss common Indigenous symbols (animals, nature, geometric patterns) and their meanings.
  • Art-Making Project:
    • Option A: Design and create a paper version of a bentwood box, drum, or talking stick, incorporating meaningful symbols. Download Bentwood Box Paper Template
    • Option B: Beadwork pattern design on graph paper (can use pony beads if available).
  • Respect and Protocols: Talk about the importance of respectful use and understanding of cultural practices.
  • Sharing Circle: Students share their artwork and explain the meaning behind their symbols.


Session 3: Songs and Dances Inspired by Tradition

Materials: Drumming circles, songs (approved recordings), Book - Secret of the Dance, space for movement

Focus: Songs and dances used in ceremonies and daily life

  • Song Learning: Teach a simple welcome song or honour song (with permission from the community or with an Indigenous Education Worker).
  • Drumming and Rhythm: Explore basic drumming patterns, using hand drums or clapping.
  • Dance Exploration: Learn simple dance steps (e.g., respectful versions of the Jingle Dress or Hoop Dance).
  • Cultural Understanding: Explain the significance of dances in healing, celebration, and storytelling.
  • Reflection: Students journal or discuss how it felt to participate in these activities.

Session 4: Storytelling and Oral Traditions

Materials: Storybooks (e.g., How Raven Stole the Sun, Shi Shi Etko, Shin Chi's Canoe, Secret of the Dance), drawing/writing supplies

Focus: Traditional stories and their role in teaching values and history

  • Story Circle: Read or listen to a traditional story from a local Indigenous Nation (ensure permission if needed).
  • Story Retelling: Students retell the story in their own words, through drawings, drama, writing, story stones.
  • Story Creation: Students create their own story inspired by the themes and teachings from the Indigenous story.

Session 5: Celebration and Sharing

Materials: Display area, performance space

Focus: Sharing learning and creations

  • Art Show/Performance: Display student artwork, invite parents/guardians, and perform songs or dances learned.
  • Reflection and Gratitude: Discuss the learning journey, and express gratitude to any Indigenous guests or knowledge keepers involved.
  • Feedback and Next Steps: Encourage students to think about how they can continue learning about Indigenous cultures respectfully.

Assessment & Extension

Assessment:

  • Observation of participation and engagement
  • Reflection journals and class discussions
  • Completed art projects and presentations
  • Respect shown for cultural protocols

Extensions:

  • Field trip to a local cultural center or museum
  • Collaborative projects with Indigenous artists
  • Deeper inquiry into local Nationsâ€™ specific traditions and practices

Resources:

  • Children's books on Indigenous Persepctive & Nature
  • Indigenous Elders or Knowledge Keepers (if available and willing)
  • Local Indigenous cultural centers
  • BC First Peoples Arts & Culture resources
  • Authentic Indigenous books and stories
  • Videos from Indigenous communities (with permissions)

Introducing students to Indigenous arts and cultural traditions is essential for fostering respect, understanding, and connection to the rich histories and contributions of Indigenous Peoples. It helps students appreciate diverse worldviews, deepens their knowledge of local communities, and supports reconciliation efforts. Engaging with traditional and contemporary Indigenous art formsâ€”such as stories, songs, dances, and craftsâ€”encourages creativity while honoring cultural significance and protocols.
